## Thoughts

"Ideal student learning experience" and "ideal employee mission fulfillment experience" are confirmed official SLCC360 institutional language -- not informal phrasing. Source: https://www.slcc.edu/slcc-360/breakout-session.aspx. The SLCC360 breakout sessions page references "SLCC's Ideal Student Learning Experience" explicitly as a session theme. Using these phrases in the one-pager anchors the argument in college-wide institutional strategy, not just Contact Center framing.

The Vision Matrix (https://www.slcc.edu/vision-matrix/index.aspx) is the active 18-month strategic plan launched January 2025. Three goals: 50,000 annual enrollments, 50% completion rate, $5,000 increase in median earnings. The Support strategy is directly relevant: "Increase the number of students receiving academic and career advising" with stated intermediate outcomes of "increased utilization of academic and career advising services" and "increased student retention." The Contact Center triage model is a direct operational implementation of the Support strategy. This is a stronger institutional anchor than SLCC360 for the one-pager because it is the current, active plan with named board-level accountability.

The institutional mission statement (https://www.slcc.edu/about/mission-vision.aspx): "We engage and support students in educational pathways leading to successful transfer and meaningful employment." IVR friction is a direct contradiction of this mission at the most basic point of contact. Students who cannot navigate the phone architecture cannot access engagement or support. This framing is available for the one-pager opening without overstating causality.

Working through a full year of PBX call data for SLCC. All call records pulled and combined into a single Excel workbook using Power Query for cleanup and row consolidation. Currently focused on calculating average duration of time spent within IVRs and queues -- the overhead layer before a caller ever reaches a person.

Methodology: calculate average duration per IVR and queue, multiply by call volume per queue over 365 days, sum across all queues to produce total overhead time imposed on callers. This becomes the core friction metric -- concrete, quantifiable time lost before a student reaches a person. Framing: wasted time, not abstract routing complexity.

Power Query data model: two tables -- fact_call_legs (one row per call leg, 1.35M rows total across all call types) and fact_calls (referenced from fact_call_legs, then grouped by Call ID to produce one row per unique call). Using Reference rather than Duplicate keeps the dependency live -- changes to fact_call_legs propagate automatically. Group By evaluation running now; unique call count unknown until complete but will be materially less than 1.35M. Note: the per-queue pivot shared earlier summed to 415,706 legs -- the 1.35M raw row count likely includes all call types (inbound, internal, outbound, tandem) before any queue-level filtering.

Current aggregate overhead figure: 43 seconds per-leg average across all 415,706 PBX legs (grand total from raw leg table). Travis's 55-second figure is likely calculated on a filtered subset (IVRs and queues only, excluding direct extensions and short-duration routing rows). Both figures are pre-dedup on unique calls -- transfer depth multiplier still to be applied.

Additional overhead layer: after an agent picks up and does a warm transfer, the receiving department's ring time is added on top of the 55-second baseline. Every transfer leg compounds the overhead. Staff making warm transfers absorb this cost on every handoff -- previously invisible, now measurable.

Present the total overhead two ways: raw time (hours, minutes, seconds) and a humanized equivalent ("over X years of student time lost to system overhead annually"). The humanized framing makes the scale visceral for a leadership audience -- it reframes a technical metric as a student impact statement.

Queue 4073 baseline clarification: 4073 is a direct transfer with no IVR in front. Its recorded wait time is ring time on the agent's side, not system-imposed overhead. This means the baseline is not zero -- it includes natural ring time -- but it is still a clean comparison point because that time is agent-driven, not architecture-driven. Worth noting this distinction in the one-pager so the comparison is defensible.

PBX call volume raw row counts (pre-dedup): 801,038 inbound, 372,976 internal, 181,333 outbound, 156 tandems. These are row counts, not unique call counts -- each call leg is its own row. Must group by Call ID before using any of these as volume figures. Numbers will drop materially once deduped. Do not cite raw row counts in the one-pager.

Key data points for the one-pager: 106 IVR menus and 26 queues currently configured in the SLCC PBX. Contact Center queue (4073) has approximately 5 seconds of overhead -- not a menu, just a spoken hold prompt before connecting. Every other department queue has a true IVR menu that takes 25 to 47 seconds to navigate on average. That gap is the core of the friction argument.

The friction problem is twofold. Students experience added IVR wait time before reaching anyone. Staff doing warm transfers are now also exposed to destination department queue wait times -- the same 25-47 second IVR overhead, on every handoff. Warm transfers were the right call for student experience, but they surfaced a cost that was previously invisible: agent time absorbed by the architecture during every transfer.

The website is a structural root cause. SLCC publishes direct department and personnel phone numbers publicly. Students call the wrong number, get transferred, wait through IVR menus again, have to re-explain their situation at each leg, and still may not reach the right place. The problem is not just overhead time -- it is energy expenditure: the repeated cognitive and relational cost imposed on students and staff to get a single need resolved. Reducing that expenditure is as important as reducing the raw wait time.

Re-explanation as extended call duration: every time a student has to re-explain their situation at a new transfer destination, that is measurable additional call time -- not just a qualitative frustration. Students are also expected to self-navigate the institution's phone architecture and know who handles what. When they inevitably arrive at the wrong place, the pattern compounds: explain, transfer, explain, transfer, explain again. Each leg adds time, adds friction, and adds frustration.

Frustration as a retention risk: this is not just an operational efficiency problem. Repeated friction in accessing support becomes a barrier to getting help at all. A student who gives up on navigating the phone system may give up on resolving the underlying issue. That path leads directly to stop-outs, drop-outs, and non-retention. The phone architecture is a student success issue, not just a telecom issue.

## Decisions

- The IVR friction one-pager is document one of a two-document package. Document two proposes renaming and restructuring the Contact Center. Both go together to AVP/VP and director level for sign-off. The pairing is intentional: the friction doc establishes the problem and institutional stakes; the Contact Center proposal delivers the solution and the structural change required to implement it. Neither document works as well alone.
- Present total IVR/queue overhead in dual format: raw cumulative time + human-scale equivalent (e.g., "equivalent to over a year of continuous wait time"). Raw for credibility, humanized for impact.
- Use queue 4073 ([[Contact Center]] direct queue, no IVR in front) as the clean baseline. Compare average total wait time for IVR-routed calls against 4073's wait time to quantify the overhead premium students pay when calling IVR-fronted numbers.
- One-pager carries a dual argument: (1) students face measurable friction from 106 IVR menus and 26 queues across the PBX, and (2) routing students through the [[Contact Center]] first acts as a triage layer that reduces inbound pressure on department staff. Friction is the problem; Contact Center as triage is the solution.
- Core recommendation: transition from Open Access to Guided Access. The [[Contact Center]] becomes the single point of entry for all people contacting the college. The Contact Center triages, resolves, or routes each caller to the appropriate place. This requires: removal of department and personnel phone numbers from the published website, routing changes in the PBX, IVR menu changes and removals. The outcome is an improvement to both the ideal student learning experience and the ideal employee mission fulfillment experience.
- Frame re-explanation as extended call duration, not just qualitative frustration. Each transfer leg where a student re-explains is measurable additional time -- makes the human cost quantifiable alongside the IVR overhead numbers.
- Escalate the argument to student success: phone friction is not an operational inconvenience, it is a retention risk. Frustration that blocks access to support can result in stop-outs, drop-outs, and non-retention. This reframes the one-pager as a student success document, not just a telecom efficiency report.

## Ideas

- Use [[Non-Returning Student Survey 2024]] as institutional corroboration for the confusion-to-exit argument. Cite DSA's own language: "making information more accessible would likely be viable interventions for decreasing early exit." Reduces IVR friction = directly implements that recommendation without overclaiming a causal attrition link.

- [[Bruin Connect Transition]] as the proposed name and structural replacement for the Contact Center. "Bruin" is the college mascot -- name change signals institutional identity alignment, not just an operational rename. Three sub-teams under the BruinConnect umbrella:
  - **Assist**: replaces the current inbound team. Reactive, handles incoming contacts. Serves the general unrepresented student population.
  - **Persist**: replaces the current outbound team. Proactive, makes outbound contacts for retention and outreach. Same population scope as Assist.
  - **Coaching**: [[Online Success Coaching]]. Holistic, relationship-based. Assigned caseload of approximately 400 students per semester. Distinct from Assist and Persist in that students have a named coach and an ongoing relationship.
  - The Assist/Persist population is explicitly the students who do NOT have a specialized CARE team assigned. Not veterans (have their own center), not international students (have their own office), not OSC coaching cohort, not academic probation programs, not TRIO. All of the unrepresented students -- the general student body who would otherwise fall through the gaps between specialized programs.
  - This framing is important: BruinConnect is the safety net for the majority of students who have no dedicated support structure. The philosophical shift is from reactive phone-answering to proactive, structured student support -- with a brand that signals ownership and institutional identity.
