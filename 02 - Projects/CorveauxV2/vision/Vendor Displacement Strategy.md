# Vendor Displacement Strategy

## The Premise

Corveaux does not begin by replacing systems.

Corveaux begins by understanding the institution.

Replacement occurs as a consequence of institutional understanding, not as the initial objective.

The long-term opportunity is not creating another software category.

The long-term opportunity is reducing the number of categories an institution must operate.

---

## Why Replacement Happens

Most institutional software models a domain.

A CMS models content.

A CRM models relationships.

A ticketing platform models requests.

A catalog platform models academic content.

An identity system models authentication.

Each system creates another representation of institutional reality.

As the number of representations grows, institutional complexity grows with it.

Corveaux reduces complexity by maintaining a canonical institutional model beneath those domains.

---

## The Sequence

### Phase 1 — Knowledge Layer

Corveaux models institutional reality.

Existing systems remain in place.

Risk remains low.

Trust is established.

---

### Phase 2 — Governance Layer

Ownership, stewardship, and institutional accountability move into Corveaux.

The institution begins governing itself through the platform.

---

### Phase 3 — Experience Layer

Websites, catalogs, directories, assistants, and knowledge experiences become projections of the canonical model.

Specialized systems become increasingly redundant.

---

### Phase 4 — Operational Layer

Workflow, communication, service delivery, and approvals begin operating directly against institutional reality.

Additional software categories become unnecessary.

---

### Phase 5 — Institutional Operating System

The institution operates through a single coherent model.

Domain-specific systems become optional rather than required.

---

## Final Principle

Corveaux does not replace software through migration.

Corveaux replaces software through convergence.
