# Founder Letter

I've spent a lot of time thinking about why Corveaux exists, and the truth is that I still struggle to answer that question in a single sentence.

The easy answer is technology. The easy answer is student success, communications, automation, knowledge management, or any of the countless categories that people naturally place software companies into. Those answers aren't wrong, but they don't really explain why I felt compelled to build this company. They explain what Corveaux does. They don't explain why it matters to me.

When I think about why Corveaux exists, I think about people.

Throughout my career, I've had the opportunity to work alongside some truly incredible individuals. Some of the most caring, hardworking, and selfless people I've ever met work in higher education. These are people who answer emails after hours because they know a student is anxious. People who stay late to help someone register for classes. People who spend their lunch break walking a student across campus because they aren't sure where to go. They care deeply about the work they do, and more importantly, they care deeply about the people they serve.

What always stood out to me wasn't a lack of effort.

It was how much effort was being spent overcoming complexity.

The longer I worked in higher education, the more I realized that institutions are filled with people trying to do the right thing while navigating systems that make doing the right thing harder than it should be. Information lives in one place, ownership lives in another, and the answer a student needs often exists somewhere in between. Entire departments spend enormous amounts of time coordinating, reconciling, searching, and translating information between systems that were never really designed to work together.

What made this especially apparent to me was the nature of the work I found myself doing. Throughout my career, and particularly within higher education, I was rarely confined to a single department, process, or system. Instead, I often found myself operating at the intersections. One day I might be working with student services. The next, IT. The next, academic affairs, enrollment, financial aid, advising, communications, or institutional policy. I became accustomed to moving between departments, systems, and organizational boundaries in ways that many people never have the opportunity to experience.

Over time, this afforded me a unique perspective on how institutions actually function. Most employees see the organization from where they sit. They understand their department, their responsibilities, and the systems they use every day. There is nothing wrong with that; in fact, it is necessary. However, when you spend enough time moving between departments and helping solve problems that span organizational boundaries, patterns begin to emerge.

I started noticing that many of the challenges institutions faced were not isolated problems. The same issues would appear repeatedly in different forms. A communication issue in one department often had roots in a process issue somewhere else. A student support challenge might ultimately trace back to ownership ambiguity. A policy that made perfect sense in one system created confusion in another. What initially appeared to be separate problems often turned out to be symptoms of the same underlying issue: the institution lacked a coherent understanding of itself.

The more I observed these patterns, the more difficult they became to ignore. I realized that I was not simply looking at individual systems, policies, or departments. I was observing the effectiveness of the institution as a whole. I was seeing where information stalled, where ownership became unclear, where knowledge became trapped, and where people were forced to compensate for shortcomings in the systems around them. More importantly, I was seeing how often dedicated employees overcame those shortcomings through sheer determination.

One of the most emotional experiences of my professional life was sitting through graduation.

I expected to feel proud. I expected to feel excited. What I didn't expect was how emotional it would be.

As student after student crossed the stage, I found myself thinking about everything that had happened before that moment. Every graduate represented years of effort, sacrifice, setbacks, victories, and persistence. But they also represented something else. Behind every student walking across that stage were dozens of people who had helped them get there.

An advisor who helped them understand a degree requirement.

A financial aid specialist who helped them stay enrolled when things became difficult.

A faculty member who encouraged them not to give up.

A staff member who answered a question at exactly the right time.

A success coach who checked in when nobody else did.

Thousands of interactions that nobody in the audience would ever see. 

Thousands of people trying to help students achieve their goals. To attain better lives, better jobs, maybe overcome things in their past. Maybe a mid-life career change for some, or give an early start to others. Thousands of people all committed to trying to help everyone they can to be the best version of themselves they can be. 

As I watched those students receive their diplomas, I couldn't help but think about how remarkable it was that so many people had found a way to help them succeed despite the complexity that surrounded them. Despite the disconnected systems. Despite the fragmented processes. Despite the bureaucracy. Despite the fact that the institution often seemed to make serving students more difficult than it needed to be.

People found a way.

They always seem to.

But how many didn't? How many stopped out because they didn't get the information they needed in time? How many stopped because they didn't know support existed? How many just didn't feel cared for? 

How many just got frustrated and gave up? 

And that's what made the experience emotional for me.

Because while I was incredibly proud of the students and the employees who helped them get there, I couldn't stop thinking about how much harder the journey had been than it should have been.

How many questions took longer to answer than necessary. 

How many students were transferred between offices.

How many employees spent their afternoons searching for information instead of helping people.

How many problems were solved through determination rather than design.

How many outcomes depended on finding the right person at the right time.

The students succeeded anyway.

The employees showed up anyway.

The institution found a way anyway.

But I left that graduation ceremony with a question that I couldn't shake.

What if it didn't have to be that hard?

What if institutions weren't forced to rely on heroics to function effectively?

What if students didn't need to understand organizational charts and departmental boundaries just to get help?

What if employees could spend less time navigating systems and more time serving people?

What if knowledge didn't disappear when someone retired?

What if ownership was clear?

What if communication was intentional?

What if the institution understood itself well enough that the people inside it could focus on the mission instead of the machinery?

The more I thought about those questions, the more I realized that many of the challenges institutions face aren't really technology problems. They are continuity problems. They are knowledge problems. They are coordination problems. They are the natural consequence of organizations becoming more complex than the systems designed to support them.

Corveaux exists because I believe institutions deserve better.

Not because I think technology is the answer to everything. Quite the opposite. I think technology has spent decades asking institutions to adapt themselves to software. Corveaux exists because I believe software should adapt itself to institutions instead.

At its core, Corveaux is built on a simple belief: people should be able to focus on people.

Students should be able to focus on learning.

Faculty should be able to focus on teaching.

Staff should be able to focus on helping.

Institutions should be able to focus on fulfilling their mission.

The systems underneath them should carry more of the burden.

I don't believe students should have to be experts in institutional bureaucracy to receive support. I don't believe employees should have to become walking databases to be effective. I don't believe institutional knowledge should disappear every time someone leaves. Most of all, I don't believe institutions should depend on luck.

The people inside our institutions are already doing extraordinary work. I've seen it firsthand. I've watched them overcome obstacles, solve problems, and change lives every single day.

Corveaux exists because I believe they deserve systems that work just as hard as they do.

Over the years, I've come to believe that some of the most important moments in a person's life are often shaped by interactions that seem insignificant at the time. A conversation with an advisor. An encouraging email from an instructor. A financial aid counselor taking a few extra minutes to explain an option that a student didn't know existed. A staff member deciding that a problem is worth one more phone call, one more email, or one more attempt to help.

Most people will never know how many lives have been changed by those moments.

I believe that one interaction can alter the trajectory of a person's life.

I believe that one conversation can give someone the confidence to keep going.

I believe that one person taking the time to care can create opportunities that might not have existed otherwise.

Higher education is full of those moments.

It is full of people who dedicate their careers to creating them.

My hope for Corveaux has never been to replace those people.

If anything, it is the opposite.

My hope is that we can remove some of the complexity that stands between people and the work they feel called to do.

That we can preserve knowledge that would otherwise be lost.

That we can make support easier to find.

That we can make ownership easier to understand.

That we can help institutions spend less time navigating themselves and more time serving the people who depend on them.

At the end of the day, technology is just a tool.

People are the point.

And if Corveaux succeeds, I hope it succeeds because it helped more people create those moments that change lives.


— Travis Hornbuckle
Founder & Chief Systems Officer, Corveaux
June 2026
