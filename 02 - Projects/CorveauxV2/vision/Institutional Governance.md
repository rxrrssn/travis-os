# Institutional Governance

## Institutions Govern Themselves

Corveaux does not govern institutions.

Institutions govern themselves.

Corveaux provides the structures through which governance can be expressed, observed, and preserved.

Governance belongs to the institution.

The platform exists to make it visible.

---

## Stewardship Over Ownership

Ownership implies possession.

Stewardship implies responsibility.

Institutions do not own knowledge.

They steward it.

A policy has a steward.

A service has a steward.

A program has a steward.

A department has a steward.

Stewardship identifies who is responsible for ensuring institutional reality remains accurate.

Stewardship is accountability, not authority.

---

## Authority Is Earned By Structure

Authority does not appear because a permission was checked.

Authority emerges from relationships, policies, and time.

A department chair has authority because the institution says they do.

A registrar has authority because governance structures grant it.

A provost has authority because authority has been delegated to the role.

Authority is institutional reality.

Permissions are merely projections of that reality.

---

## Governance Requires Memory

Institutions often remember decisions but forget why they were made.

A policy survives.

The context disappears.

A process survives.

The rationale disappears.

A committee approves something.

Years later nobody remembers who approved it or why.

Governance without memory becomes tradition.

Tradition without explanation becomes confusion.

Corveaux preserves governance history alongside governance decisions.

The institution remembers not only what happened, but why.

---

## Delegation Is A Relationship

Authority may be delegated.

Responsibility may be delegated.

Work may be delegated.

Accountability may not disappear.

Delegation is not an exception.

Delegation is an institutional relationship.

It can be modeled.

It can be observed.

It can be audited.

---

## Temporary Reality Is Still Reality

Acting appointments exist.

Interim leadership exists.

Committees exist.

Working groups exist.

Emergency authorities exist.

Institutions constantly create temporary structures in order to function.

Temporary does not mean informal.

Temporary structures should be represented with the same rigor as permanent ones.

Time determines validity.

Not permanence.

---

## Every Decision Leaves A Trace

Institutional decisions should not disappear.

Policy approvals.

Governance changes.

Delegations.

Appointments.

Exceptions.

Approvals.

Every decision becomes part of institutional history.

The objective is not surveillance.

The objective is continuity.

Future institutions deserve to understand how current institutions arrived where they are.

---

## Governance Is A First-Class Institutional Function

Governance is not overhead.

Governance is how institutions coordinate action across time.

Without governance:

- Authority becomes ambiguous.
- Ownership becomes unclear.
- Knowledge becomes fragmented.
- Accountability disappears.

Institutions do not become simpler as they grow.

They become more dependent on governance.

---

## Continuity Is The Goal

The purpose of governance is not control.

The purpose of governance is continuity.

The institution should survive personnel changes.

The institution should survive reorganizations.

The institution should survive leadership transitions.

The institution should survive time.

People change.

Institutions endure.

Governance is how continuity is preserved.

---

## The Governance Principle

Every governance decision must pass a single test:

> Does this make the institution easier or harder to understand, operate, and preserve over time?

If it makes continuity harder, it is probably the wrong decision.

If it strengthens continuity, it is probably the right one.
