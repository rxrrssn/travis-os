## The Observation

One of the things that has stood out to me throughout my career is how difficult it can be for institutions to understand themselves.

When most people think about colleges and universities, they think about students, faculty, classrooms, and degrees. What they don't often see is the number of systems, processes, departments, and people that all have to work together to make those experiences possible.

In many cases, everyone is trying to do the right thing.

The problem is that they are often doing it in different places.

One office updates a website.

Another updates a CRM.

Another updates a spreadsheet.

Another updates a knowledge base.

None of these decisions are made with bad intentions, but over time they create an environment where nobody is completely sure which version of information is correct.

---

## The Consequence

When information becomes fragmented, people begin spending time looking for answers instead of helping students.

Questions that should have simple answers become difficult to resolve.

Ownership becomes unclear.

Knowledge becomes dependent on specific employees.

Students are transferred between departments because nobody is entirely sure who is responsible.

Most institutions do not have a people problem.

Most institutions do not have a software problem.

They have a coordination problem.

---

## Why Corveaux Exists

When I started thinking about how these problems could be solved, I realized that most solutions focus on adding another system to the environment.

The institution ends up with another login.

Another dashboard.

Another database.

Another source of truth.

That approach never felt right to me.

Instead of adding another disconnected system, I believe institutions need a way to understand themselves as a whole.

Corveaux exists because I believe institutions deserve that opportunity.

---

## The Goal

The goal is not to replace people.

The goal is not to automate every decision.

The goal is to reduce the amount of effort institutions spend reconciling themselves.

The more time employees spend fighting systems, the less time they spend helping students.

If the system can do more of the administrative work, people can focus more of their attention on the people they serve.

That is ultimately why Corveaux exists.
