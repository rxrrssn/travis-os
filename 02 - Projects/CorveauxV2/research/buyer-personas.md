# Buyer Personas  
  
**Status:** Complete. Researched 2026-06-06.  
  
## Purpose  
  
Identify who experiences the pain Corveaux addresses, who champions change internally, who controls budget, and who ultimately approves adoption.  
  
The initial go-to-market buyer may not be the long-term platform buyer.  
  
This document captures hypotheses to validate through institutional research and customer conversations.  
  
---  
  
## Persona Types  
  
### Economic Buyer  
  
Controls budget, procurement, and contract approval.  
  
May not experience the operational pain directly.  
  
### Champion  
  
Believes the problem exists, advocates internally, builds consensus, and helps navigate institutional politics.  
  
May or may not control budget.  
  
### User  
  
Experiences the pain directly and provides requirements, feedback, and validation.  
  
### Approver  
  
Possesses formal authority to authorize procurement, strategic initiatives, or institutional change.  
  
At some institutions this is the same person as the economic buyer. At others it is not.  
  
---  
  
## Primary Buyer Hypothesis  
  
### CIO / VP of Strategic Initiatives  
  
Hypothesis:  
  
The initial Corveaux buyer is likely a leader whose authority crosses organizational boundaries and who can justify solving problems that span:  
  
- Website governance  
- Catalog management  
- Knowledge management  
- Student experience  
- Enrollment journeys  
- Institutional operations  
  
Corveaux's initial value proposition crosses silos. The buyer must therefore have cross-silo authority.  
  
### Pain Signals  
  
- Multiple disconnected institutional systems  
- Repeated content duplication  
- Inconsistent information across surfaces  
- Website governance problems  
- Catalog maintenance burden  
- Knowledge ownership ambiguity  
- AI initiatives without institutional structure  
- Difficulty understanding institutional relationships and responsibilities  
  
### Success Metrics  
  
- Reduced content duplication  
- Improved governance visibility  
- Faster information updates  
- Improved institutional consistency  
- Reduced operational friction

## Institutional Purchasing Questions  
  
### Procurement  
  
- [x] What is the approval path for a $40,000 annual contract? — **VP-level approval.** Typically VP of Technology or VP of Administrative Services. 4–12 week cycle. Procurement office review required (kicks in at $10K–$15K). Informal competitive quotes (2–3 vendors) likely required even without a formal RFP. HECVAT security questionnaire mandatory for any SaaS. Legal review triggered by presence of a vendor contract, not by dollar threshold.

- [x] What is the approval path for a $100,000 annual contract? — **VP + President-level; possible Board of Trustees action.** Community college Boards often have approval thresholds at $75K–$100K depending on state or local policy. At SLCC-scale, a $100K SaaS contract likely requires: VP recommendation → President approval → possible Board ratification. Timeline: 4–6 months including procurement. An RFP process adds another 3–6 months on top.

- [x] Which approvals require procurement review? — **Kicks in at $10K–$15K** depending on institution. Above threshold: procurement office issues or reviews the purchase order, requires vendor registration, and may trigger cooperative purchasing verification (OMNIA, E&I, Sourcewell). Anything above $25K typically requires at least 3 quotes. Above $50K: formal competitive solicitation or documented sole-source justification.

- [x] Which approvals require legal review? — **Any vendor contract triggers legal review,** regardless of dollar amount. Not threshold-driven — the presence of a contract (MSA, SaaS agreement, DPA, or addendum) is sufficient. FERPA data handling terms and HECVAT security questionnaire are standard requirements for any system touching student data. State institutions may require an additional state attorney general review for multi-year contracts.

### Governance  
  
- [x] Who can approve implementation without Faculty Senate involvement? — **Administrative and operational systems with no instructional impact** can be approved at the VP/CIO level without Faculty Senate. Examples: document management, procurement, HR tools, institutional research tools. If the system touches curriculum, course content, grading, or advising logic — Faculty Senate review is expected. Corveaux positioned as a knowledge extraction layer (reads approved facts, doesn't generate curriculum) avoids Faculty Senate involvement for the initial deployment.

- [x] What initiatives require cabinet-level approval? — **Cross-functional technology initiatives, multi-year strategic commitments, and anything above VP budget authority** require cabinet-level endorsement. Cabinet = President + VP team (Academic Affairs, Student Affairs, Finance, Technology, HR). Initiatives that touch multiple offices or require policy changes move through cabinet. For Corveaux at $40K: VP-level only. For an institutional-wide deployment at $150K+: cabinet buy-in required.

- [x] How are cross-functional initiatives governed? — At community colleges, cross-functional tech initiatives are governed through the **Technology or IT Governance Committee** (CIO-chaired), which filters recommendations to cabinet. Faculty governance (Academic Senate/Faculty Senate) controls anything touching instruction. New tools that span two or more VP areas typically need both the IT Governance Committee sign-off and the sponsoring VP's budget authority. Without a presidential champion, cross-functional projects stall.

### Budget  
  
- [x] What discretionary technology budgets exist? — **CIO typically controls $10K–$50K in annual operational discretion** for software tools, licenses, and small IT purchases without competitive procurement. Beyond that, budget requests must go through the annual cycle (proposals due January–March for July 1 fiscal year start). Some institutions have a "technology innovation" or "strategic initiative" fund ($25K–$100K) controlled by the President or VP of Finance for out-of-cycle priorities.

- [x] Who controls them? — **CIO** controls IT operational discretion. **VP of Academic Affairs** controls instructional technology budgets. If a tool can be framed as either IT or instructional technology, the buying conversation depends on who initiates. Corveaux at the knowledge extraction / institutional intelligence layer most naturally lands with the CIO. Advisee-facing features may shift the conversation toward Academic Affairs.

- [x] How are innovation projects funded? — Primarily through: (1) **reallocation within an existing VP budget**, (2) **presidential strategic initiative funds** (rare, competitive), or (3) **grant funding** (Title III, Perkins, state workforce grants). Community colleges rarely have dedicated "innovation budgets." The most common path is a VP who believes in the tool using operational flexibility to fund a pilot ($15K–$40K), with expansion contingent on demonstrating ROI before the next budget cycle.

## Research Questions  
  
### Buyer Discovery  
  
- [x] Who initiates these conversations at SLCC-type institutions? — **CIO or VP of Technology** is the most common initiator for AI/knowledge management tools. VP of Academic Affairs initiates for instructional-adjacent tools. In community colleges with a dedicated VP of Strategic Initiatives or VP of Innovation (rare but growing), that role may drive the conversation. Conversations rarely bubble up from below VP level — budget authority and cross-functional reach are prerequisites for initiating.

- [x] Who owns the pain today? — Pain ownership is split: **Web Director** owns content governance pain (stale pages, ownership ambiguity, duplication). **Registrar** owns catalog maintenance pain (annual update burden, currency gaps). **CIO** owns integration fragmentation pain (too many systems, none talking to each other). **Director of Institutional Effectiveness** owns institutional visibility pain (can't answer accreditation questions quickly, data is in too many places). The buyer conversation depends on which pain surface Corveaux is pitched against.

- [x] Who controls budget? — **CIO** for IT and operational technology. **VP Academic Affairs** for instructional technology and advising tools. **VP Student Affairs** for student experience tools. At SLCC-scale, the CIO is the most likely budget owner for Corveaux's entry wedge (knowledge extraction + institutional intelligence). Dual-budget situations (CIO + Academic Affairs both need to agree) are common for tools that span instruction and operations.

- [x] Who signs contracts? — **VP-level** for contracts under $75K–$100K. **President** for contracts above that threshold or multi-year commitments above VP authority. The signing authority is set by Board policy. For community colleges governed by a Board of Trustees, contracts above a stated threshold require Board ratification — discovery call objective is always to understand this threshold early.

- [x] Who says no? — The five veto players at a community college: (1) **Board of Trustees** — if the dollar amount exceeds their approval threshold; (2) **Faculty Senate** — if the tool has even perceived instructional impact; (3) **IT security** — if HECVAT or data handling terms fail review; (4) **Legal/Counsel** — if contract terms are non-standard or data privacy terms are unclear; (5) **Procurement** — if proper competitive process was not followed. The CIO can also say no to any tool that creates an integration burden.

### Champion Discovery  
  
- [x] Who is the internal champion? — Most likely: **Web Director**, **Institutional Effectiveness Coordinator**, or a **Strategic Initiatives staff member** who experiences fragmentation daily. At some institutions, a frustrated Registrar or Advising Director becomes the champion because they're closest to the data accuracy problem. Champions are operationally embedded — they experience the pain, they can articulate it, but they rarely have the budget authority to approve it themselves. Their role is to build internal consensus and bring the conversation to the economic buyer.

- [x] What motivates them? — Relief from a specific daily friction: updating the catalog every year, reconciling program information across three systems, answering the same institutional questions manually, finding content errors before accreditation reviewers do. Champions are motivated by fixing something that has always been broken — not by "AI" generically. Frame Corveaux as the tool that makes their job dramatically easier, not as institutional transformation.

- [x] What risks do they perceive? — (1) **Political exposure** if the new tool fails or creates problems for other offices; (2) **change management burden** — being responsible for driving adoption across colleagues who didn't ask for change; (3) **IT integration complexity** — fear that connecting a new tool will create work for IT they'll be blamed for; (4) **cost-justification pressure** — having to demonstrate ROI to their VP to justify continuing; (5) **accreditation risk** — if catalog or program information is disrupted mid-cycle.

- [x] What political capital must they spend? — **Significant at VP level, low below VP.** The champion spends political capital by: bringing the tool to the CIO's attention (bypasses their normal sphere); asking IT security to review a new vendor (creates IT work); proposing a budget reallocation (requires VP approval); and if instructional-adjacent, preparing Faculty Senate communication. The smaller the footprint (read-only, no new integration, single VP budget line), the less political capital required. This is why a $30–40K knowledge extraction pilot is the right entry point — it fits within VP discretionary authority.

### Procurement Discovery  
  
- [x] What does the typical budget authority look like at a community college? — **Tiered by dollar amount.** Operational discretion: CIO $10K–$50K. VP-level approval: up to $75K–$100K. President approval: $100K+. Board ratification: typically $75K–$100K+ (varies by institution policy). Cooperative purchasing contracts (OMNIA, Sourcewell, E&I) may bypass competitive procurement and allow VP-level approval at higher dollar amounts. Being on a cooperative vehicle removes 6–12 months of additional procurement friction.

- [x] Who can approve a purchase independently? — **CIO** within their discretion limit (typically $10K–$50K). **VP** within their budget authority (typically up to $75K–$100K). Above those thresholds, independent approval is not possible — requires additional sign-offs. Sole-source justification documentation can enable independent approval for a unique product with no competitive alternative, but these are scrutinized and rarely approved above $50K without strong documentation.

- [x] How long does procurement typically take? — **$40K annual SaaS: 4–12 weeks** from initial approval to signed contract. Steps: vendor security review (HECVAT, 2–4 weeks), legal review of MSA and DPA (2–4 weeks), procurement office processing (1–2 weeks), VP signature. Budget already exists = faster. New budget line = add one budget cycle. **$100K+: 4–6 months** minimum. RFP required: add 3–6 months. If the fiscal year has closed, add another 6–12 months for next cycle. The fastest path is a CIO who has discretionary budget and has already seen the demo.  
  
### Competitive Discovery  
  
- [x] Which incumbent vendors are already trusted? — See [[competitive-landscape]] §4. Tier 1: Ellucian, Microsoft, Canvas. Tier 2: Modern Campus or CourseLeaf, EAB/Civitas, Salesforce.
- [x] Which vendors are difficult to displace? — Ellucian Banner (SIS system of record), Canvas (LMS + faculty training investment), catalog platforms (governance-embedded). See [[competitive-landscape]] §2 on stickiness.
- [x] Which contracts are already in place? — Typical community college has 3–5 year contracts with Ellucian, Microsoft, and LMS vendor. Catalog platform contracts are 3–5 years. Procurement commonly routes through OMNIA Partners, E&I Cooperative, or Sourcewell. See [[competitive-landscape]] §4.  
  
### Strategic Discovery  
  
- [x] What problem is painful enough that a CIO will spend political capital to solve it? — **Accreditation exposure and AI accuracy risk.** A CIO will spend political capital when the alternative is an institutional failure visible to the Board or accreditors. Specific triggers: (1) HLC/SACSCOC accreditation reviewers citing catalog inaccuracies; (2) AI systems (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google) generating materially wrong information about institutional programs — at 10K+ prospective student scale, this is a reputational liability the CIO can point to; (3) FERPA-adjacent data consistency issues across systems. Pain framing: "Your AI reputation is already being set by systems you don't control."

- [x] What problem is painful enough that a cabinet-level leader will sponsor it? — **Institutional consistency at scale** — when a cabinet member personally experiences or is called to account for inconsistent information across the institution. The Provost cares when accreditors or the Board can't get consistent answers about program inventory. The President cares when AI-generated prospective student answers are wrong and it appears in local media or enrollment numbers are affected. The entry framing for cabinet: "We can show you exactly what AI systems believe about your institution right now, and what's wrong." The demonstration is the sponsorship catalyst.

- [x] Which problems create urgency versus interest? — **Urgency:** Active accreditation cycle (HLC self-study or reaffirmation); discovery that AI search tools are generating wrong program information; pending catalog publication deadline (annual May–June window); Board or administrative pressure to demonstrate AI governance. **Interest (not urgency):** Content governance improvement; reducing catalog update burden; institutional knowledge fragmentation. Lead with urgency surfaces, validate interest surfaces as expansion opportunities. The Day 30 Gate generates a real urgency artifact: "Here is what ChatGPT says about your programs. Here is what is correct."

### Platform Discovery  
  
- [x] Does the buyer see Corveaux as software, infrastructure, or institutional capability? — **Initially: software** (a tool that does a specific job). The entry wedge buyer (CIO, Web Director) categorizes Corveaux as a knowledge extraction / AI-readiness tool — a software purchase. The framing shifts to **infrastructure** when the buyer realizes the canonical model is the authoritative layer other systems read from. The framing shifts to **institutional capability** when executives understand that the institutional model is the source of truth for governance, accreditation, student experience, and AI. The sales journey maps to these three framings in sequence.

- [x] When does the conversation shift from knowledge management to institutional operations? — When the buyer asks: "Can this also tell me who is responsible for a given program?" or "Can I see which offices are accountable for which policies?" — that is the shift. It happens naturally when Corveaux's canonical entity model surfaces relationships and policies that weren't visible before. The trigger is usually the demo: showing not just that program facts are accurate, but that the org structure, policy ownership, and responsibility chains are modeled canonically and queryable.

- [x] Which executive becomes the buyer once Corveaux expands beyond the entry wedge? — **COO or Provost** for the institutional operations expansion. **President or System Office VP** for multi-institution governance. The Provost owns curriculum governance, program quality, and accreditation — all of which Corveaux's canonical model directly addresses. The COO (or VP Administrative Services) owns operational efficiency across the institution. System office leadership at the USHE/community college system level becomes the buyer when multi-institution standardization is the value. The sequence: CIO (entry) → Provost + COO (expansion) → System Office (scale).

## Working Hypothesis  
  
The initial buyer is likely a CIO or equivalent cross-functional technology leader.  
  
The initial champion is likely closer to the operational pain:  
  
- Web Director  
- Institutional Effectiveness  
- Strategic Initiatives  
- Enrollment Operations  
  
The long-term platform buyer may ultimately become:  
  
- COO  
- Provost  
- President  
- System Office Leadership  
  
The initial sale and the eventual Institutional Operating System sale may involve different stakeholders.
