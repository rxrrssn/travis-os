## Talking points and framing

- **The thesis (lead with this):** these are the least expensive enrollments the college can earn, because it already earned every one once. Recruiting a new student costs marketing and months; re-opening the door for one already here costs a text. Persistence framing and fiscal framing are the same argument said once.
- **The click comparison:** Marketing pays ~$11 for an "Apply Now" click that usually never becomes a student, true cost per new enrolled student runs $100-200+. Fifteen dollars removes the last barrier for a student who already applied, enrolled, and attended.
- **The $15 one-liner:** "We spend $2,500 to enroll one new student. $1,250 total would re-enroll seventy-four."
- **Moral-hazard rebuttal (if it comes up):** the hold teaches nothing, students don't know it exists until registration fails. The fine survives as the accountability instrument, still owed, still in collections. "We're not removing the consequence, we're removing the part where we punish ourselves too."
- **Allocation-neutral (if metering comes up):** the textable filter reaches back only ~300 days, inside the current contract year, so every cohort member already consumed their unique-user unit. Re-texting them draws zero new allocation. This campaign doesn't even spend the balance you are trying to use before it resets.
- **It is his play:** this is the annual remaining-allocation stop-out spend-down, sharpened from "past semester or two, as far as quantity allows" to a precision list where every recipient is verified blocked, reachable, and non-graduated.
- **KPI tie-in:** measurable progress toward leadership's stated 50K-by-2027 enrollment goal.
