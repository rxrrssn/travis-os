# Interim Interview Model Answers

Model versions of all seven mock 02 questions, written in Travis's voice. These are routing targets, not scripts: each shows which set pieces fire on which trigger, with one number minimum and a landed last sentence. Rules obeyed: no unit costs, no exec-only material, shared credit on department wins, word patrol clean.

---

## Q1. Why interested / what would you accomplish

I started in this department, in the seats of the outbound team I now supervise. That gave me eighteen months of seeing this operation from the floor up, and the last six months building its systems alongside the director you're replacing. I want this role because the department is mid-transformation and I'm the continuity for it. If you select me, I'm committing to three things. First, stability: service levels, staffing, and systems hold steady through the search. Second, I protect the reporting migration to institutional platforms. I built that layer, and our dashboard is in the publication pipeline right now. Third, momentum: the outbound caseload redesign and the capacity work leadership has already seen keep moving. This wouldn't be a holding pattern. It would be a seamless continuation of work you've already seen, because I'm the one who started it.

*(Fires: origin story, three commitments, opener close. The "accomplish" half of the question IS the commitments.)*

## Q2. Why you, why now (tenure)

Two parts to that: tenure and timing. On tenure, my leadership experience is longer than my SLCC tenure. I've led teams of 120 end to end, and at Universal I was promoted over a team with decades inside the organization, so I know that transition works only when you address it directly. My first week here starts with one-on-ones that do exactly that. What's newer to me is SLCC's specific processes, and I've spent eighteen months inside them, including rewriting this department's policy manual against current College policy. On timing: this center is mid-transition. The reporting is moving institutional, the outreach redesign is mid-flight, and the capacity work is in front of leadership. An interim who learns those three from a binder loses the year. I'm the continuity for all three. That's why now.

*(Fires: 120 scale, Universal precedent, policy manual proof, the binder line as the close. Answers the literal question in the final two sentences.)*

## Q3. Who does your job if you move up

Directly: I do, and by design that's manageable. Three reasons. First, the work is built not to depend on me personally. Every automation is designed to run on service accounts rather than my login — that migration is sitting in IT's approval queue now — and every definition and procedure is documented and versioned in the meantime. Moving seats doesn't break anything, and the service-account cutover finishes the de-personalization. Second, the largest piece, the reporting model, is in the institutional publication pipeline right now. Once it publishes, refresh and maintenance are automated and institutionally hosted. Third, the judgment layer comes with me into the seat, which means you fill one position instead of solving two problems. The honest cost is that new development slows during the interim. Operations won't feel it. The systems are built to run.

*(Fires: literal answer first, all three mechanism beats, the honest beat, ends on evidence. "Resilient, not redundant" framing throughout.)*

## Q4. Budget experience and approach

Four contexts. At Nike I owned the budget for a 120-employee operation end to end: procurement, labor, vendor lines. Here, I manage the vendor relationship for this department's largest line item, and I'm running that renewal evaluation right now, including whether it survives against alternatives. Third, my schedule redesign is a budget tool: deploy the most expensive people where the demand model says they deliver the most, cover the hinges with lower-cost resources. And fourth, I hold my own tools to the same standard I hold vendors to. I brought a licensing line into this budget, it earned its keep while it built our documentation culture, and when the landscape changed I cut my own line item before it ever billed. Last thing: we just entered the new fiscal year, and I was part of shaping this budget as we planned for it. I wouldn't be learning this year's budget. I'd be executing one that already has my fingerprints on it.

*(Fires: four contexts, full-lifecycle TDX story, capstone ending. Zero unit costs. No "admittedly new" concession anywhere.)*

## Q5. Supervising colleagues who also applied

I'll answer from experience rather than theory. People operations aren't new to me. I owned them end to end for a 120-person team: hiring, onboarding, training plans, documentation, corrective action from first warning through termination. This department is a tenth of that scale, and these conversations are the part of the job I've done the most. So, mechanically: first week, I sit down with each of them one-on-one and name it directly, because ambiguity breeds resentment, not the outcome itself. And the frame is honest. It isn't a no, it's a not this time. Then we align on what they own, where they want to grow, and what I can do to develop them toward the next opportunity. These are colleagues I respect and want on this team long after the interim ends, and I know how to have that conversation so the relationship survives it.

*(Fires: people pillar FIRST, then the mechanism, then "not this time," ends on relationship commitment. No negative imagery in the close.)*

## Q6. Leading a fully remote frontline operation

Three layers. Standards: we just modernized the department handbook, the first major revision since 2021, and it sets explicit expectations for responsiveness and escalation. Presence is written down, not assumed. Measurement: I built this department's instrumentation from the ground up, and every function carries metrics aligned to its actual work. Coaches carry outreach and activity, inbound carries service level and queue health, outbound carries contact attempts and data quality. I don't manage a green dot on Teams. I manage a picture of the work. And third, leadership presence in a remote operation is rhythm and responsiveness, not geography. That means consistent one-on-ones and team huddles as the baseline, and during the August and January peaks it means being in the operation with the team: watching the queues live, clearing escalations, running daily check-ins while the volume is heaviest. This team runs fully remote today and the service levels prove the model works. My job is to lead it the way it actually operates, and I built the visibility layer that makes that possible.

*(Fires: the mock 02 answer that scored 4, with the third beat corrected 7/13 — the entire team is 100% remote, on-site only for all-hands, so the presence beat is virtual intensity during peaks, never "days in the building." Ends on evidence.)*

## Q7. Anything we haven't asked / why select you

Two things. First, I've referenced a lot of work today, and I brought a short brief of it: the chatbot's arc from a 61 percent match rate to 89, the demand-forecast schedule redesign, the dashboard now in institutional review, the student registration hub. I'll leave a copy with each of you. Second, why me, in two sentences. The previous director and I spent the last year redesigning this department's service strategy together. She carried it into leadership rooms, and I built the systems underneath it, so I know exactly where the transformation stands and where it's headed. Select me and the interim period isn't a handoff, it's a continuation, and I'm committed to the same three things I opened with: stability through the search, protecting the reporting migration, and momentum on the redesign. The results between now and January will speak for themselves.

*(Fires: folio handoff with one number attached, shared-build continuity, commitments recapped as bookend, evidence close.)*

## The routing table (drill this, not the scripts)

| Trigger heard | Fire |
|---|---|
| Motivation / accomplish / vision | Three commitments + opener close |
| Tenure / readiness / why you | 120 + Universal + policy manual, binder line closes |
| Your current role / replacement / spread too thin | "I do" first, three beats, honest beat |
| Budget / resources / fiscal | Four contexts, TDX lifecycle, capstone ending |
| People / conflict / fellow applicants / hard conversation | People pillar FIRST, mechanism second |
| Remote / accountability / performance | Handbook, metric stacks, peak-anchored presence |
| Close / anything else | Folio + commitments bookend + results promise |

One number minimum per answer. Last sentence is evidence or commitment. Then stop.
